National Debt in the Philippines

Comparing the admins of Presidents Benigno Aquino III and Rodrigo Duterte

Incurring national debt can be seen as necessary. The government budget is partially composed of debts received either internally or externally. However, there needs to be a careful balance between national debt and a country’s gross domestic product (GDP). One disadvantage of keeping debts high is the lower amount of money allocated to entities who need it. Money would instead be spent decreasing the country’s debt instead. Another disadvantage is the lowering of a country’s credit rating. This would disincentivize further external loans from being granted. Finally, excessive debt forces the country to keep its economy growing to further increase its GDP. While more developed nations are capable of doing so, developing countries do not have the same expectations.

As it stands, the Philippines is said to have a good credit rating according to credit agencies S&P Global, Moody’s Investor Service, and Fitch Ratings. This is despite a debt rating as high as P 13.64 Trillion recorded by the Bureau of Treasury last November 2022. This is also despite our current debt-to-GDP ratio sitting at 63.7%, exceeding what the European Union’s Stability and Growth Pact considered stable (60%) as well as Asia’s average ratio (63.5%). Going forward, it is important to manage our debt-to-GDP ratio, otherwise it puts a strain on our economy as a whole.
